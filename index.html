Poverty in Mexico: trends and challenges after Covid-19
By Sandra Valdés Salas

Introduction


As result of the recent crisis prompted by COVID-19 pandemic, the UN estimates that nearly 29 million people will fall into poverty this year in Latin America, according to the report The social challenge in times of COVID-19 recently published by the Economic Commission for Latin America and the Caribbean. Further, it is expected that Mexico, one of the countries in the region with the largest percentage of poor people (more than 40% of the country's population), will be one of the most affected countries in Latin America due to the slow response of the government to the pandemic crisis.

Poverty by country in Latin America


Despite efforts of the Mexican government to decrease poverty during the past decades, Mexico has thousand of inhabitants living under conditions of poverty. The aim of this project is to take an in depth look into poverty statistics of Mexico in order to bring insight into the social consequences of the pandemic. Then, we will take a dive into two dimensions of poverty that might be deeply affected by COVID-19: income and access to health services.

Overview of poverty trends



Mexico adopted in 2009 a multidimensional poverty measurement, becoming the first country in the world to have an official metric of this nature. According to this measurement, income and social deprivations are both important factors for poverty measurement. These social deprivations are: educational gap, lack of access to health services, lack of access to food, lack of access to social security, poor quality of the dwelling, and lack of access to basic services in the dwelling.

Accordingly, a person is classified as:
i) Poor (low income plus social deprivations)
ii) Vulnerable due to social deprivations
iii) Vulnerable due to low income
iv) Not poor and not vulnerable

To find out more about these definitions, take a look at the Methodology Notes.
For a deeper dive, visit Coneval's page(available in English).

Poverty at the national level


According to the last measurement of poverty in 2018, nearly 42% of the population in Mexico is considered poor, 29% vulnerable due to social deprivations, 22% vulnerable due to low income, and only 7% was not poor and not vulnerable. In the case of social deprivations, more than 70 thousand Mexicans do not have social security whereas more than 20 thousand people suffer from deprivation due to access to health services.


Change of poverty at the national level


Overall, the country has experienced some advances in terms of reducing poverty, but also some setbacks over a 10-year period (from 2008 to 2018). On the one hand, the population of poor people in Mexico shows a downward trend. On the other hand, the population vulnerable due to social deprivations has increased, whereas the population vulnerable due to a low income has a minimum decrease, as shown in the graph below.

Poverty at the state level


If we take a deeper look into poverty trends at the state-level, we can see that poverty has increased significantly in eight states between 2008 and 2018. In Mexico City, the second largest city in the country (with a population of 8.7 million people), the percentage of poor people increased from 27.6% to 30.6%, suggesting that urban poverty has been on the rise.

Poverty at the municipal level


At a more granular level, the Southern municipalities in Mexico are the poorest. In some of these municipalities, the entire population is poor. By contrast, the Northern region tends to have richer municipalities. However, we can also see some pockets of poverty in the Northeast region and the Center-North region.


Even though the south is the poorest region, in terms of poverty, some of the municipalities in the north show a rapid increment of poverty in just five years. Despite the fact that the Northern states have a smaller percentage of poor people at the state-level, poverty is rapidly expanding in some Northern municipalities, raising concerns for the upcoming years.


How is poverty been affected by Covid-19 in Mexico?


By the end of March, 2020, the cases of Covid-19 started to increase in Mexico. By mid-June Mexico reported around 6 thousand new cases.

Last update: July 1, 2020.

Covid-19 total cases at the municipal level


Covid-19 has expanded across the country with different levels of intensity, especially in Mexico's large cities and ports. The Central and Southern regions have reported a large number of cases, in particular due to the high density of the population.

Covid-19 impact in regions classified by level of poverty


By dividing all municipalities according to their level of poverty, we can see that those municipalities with a moderate level of poverty, are the most impacted by the pandemic: these regions concentrate most of covid-19 cases and most of the deaths caused by covid-19 (see graph below). Regions classified with a moderate level of poverty might include urban areas where societies are typically unequal in terms of income, welfare and quality of living. Regions with a moderate level of poverty will face important challenges in the upcoming months in order to avoid an increment of poverty.

Note: States with more than 75% of poor population were classified as "very high", with more than 50% but less than 75% as "high", with more than 25% but less than 50% as "moderate", and with less than 25% as "low".

Income vulnerability


Vulnerability due to income is one of the key elements considered for measuring poverty. A person is considered vulnerable due to income when he or she does not have sufficient income to buy the aggregation of the basic food basket with the basic non-food basket, but has no social deprivations. As shown in the graph below, between 2008 and 2018 there has been a steady rise of the population vulnerable due to income: over a 10 year period, the percentage of the population with a very low income has increased 17%, especially after the 2008 financial crisis and 2009 H1N1 epidemic.

Lessons from the past show the potential social effects that an epidemic can have on the population. During April 2009, Mexico was affected by the H1N1 epidemic, which caused a major slowdown of the economic activity in most cities, a total of 70 thousand people were infected and around 1 thousand people died. The H1N1 epidemic, which coincided with the effects of the 2008 financial crisis, had devastating economic implications for Mexico: the cost of the pandemic was estimated at 1% of the country's GDP in 2008, and the country suffered an increment of people vulnerale due to income. Income vulnerability by municipality in Mexico


As expected, those municipalities with a large population report many cases of people with Covid-19. If we take a closer look to income dynamics, in many of these municipalities, a large share of the population has an income that is not sufficient to buy the basic food and non-food basket. To put it simpler words, the pandemic is hitting in places (most of them urban) where many people has a very low income or where income vulnerability could easily increase from a moderate to a high level. This raises concerns about the potential effects of quarantines, the loss of employment and economic depression in cities, where a large percentage of the population is already vulnerable due to income.

Last update: July 1, 2020.
Only municipalities with confirmed Covid-19 cases were considered.

Lack of access to health services


Access to health is a social deprivation indicator which is expected to be affected deeply during and after the pandemic. Although other social deprivations are equally important (social security, educational gap, food insecurity, basic services in the dwelling, and poor quality of the dwelling), this project focuses on health services because it is expected to be immediatly affected by the pandemic. Nonetheless, all social deprivations will be affected in the long term and, therefore, future research on this area will be important to understand the social effects of Covid-19 in Mexico.


Access to health services in Mexico


In general terms, lack of access to health services has decreased abruptly between 2008 and 2018: at the national level, the percentage of the population with this social deprivation was reduced from 38% to 16% over this 10-year period. However, in only 2 years (between 2016 and 2018), more than half of the states in Mexico reported an increment of their population without access to health services .


Impact of Covid-19 in regions with deprivation of health services


A deeper look into access to health and Covid-19 dynamics, suggests that those regions classified with a very high level of deprivation of health services, report the largest number of deaths allegedly caused by Covid-19. In other words, those places that have not guaranteed access to health services for the majority of their population, are now facing a higher death rate than other places. Although this does not suggest causation, there is a relationship between deprivation of health services and deaths caused by the pandemic.

Last update: July 1, 2020.
Note: States with more than 75% of the population with deprivation of health services were classified as "very high", with more than 50% but less than 75% as "high", with more than 25% but less than 50% as "moderate", and with less than 25% as "low".

Conclusions


Poverty trends and Covid-19

From 2008 and 2018, poverty has increased significantly in some states, including Mexico City which is the second largest city in the country and has reported the largest figures of Covid-19 cases (nearly 50 thousand cases by July 1, 2020). Regions classified with a "moderate" level of poverty (many of which include middle income cities) have reported the largest figures of Covid-19 cases. This raises concerns about the challenges that middle-income municipalities will face in the upcoming months in order to avoid a rapid increment of poverty.

Income vulnerability and Covid-19

From 2008 to 2018, the percentage of the population in Mexico with a very low income increased 17%. Currently, those municipalities (primarily urban) that have more than a 1 thousand cases of Covid-19, also have a large population of people with a very low salary. This raises concerns about the impact of the pandemic in regions, mostly urban areas, where the population is already vulnerable due to income.

Deprivation of health services and Covid-19

Finally, between 2016 and 2018, more than half of the states in Mexico reported an increment of their population without access to health services. Currently, those regions classified with a very high level of deprivation of health services, also report the largest number of deaths allegedly caused by Covid-19. This raises concerns about the impact of the pandemic in regions where the population has previously faced limitations to access health services.

Methodology Notes

Mexico's multidimensional poverty measurement takes into account both income and social deprivations. Accordingly, a person is classified as:
  • Poor
  • Vulnerable due to social deprivations
  • Vulnerable due to income
  • Not poor and not vulnerable
Definitions
  • Poor person:A person is living in poverty when he/she experiences one or more social deprivations and does not have sufficient income to buy the aggregation of the basic food basket with the basic non-food basket.
  • Vulnerable person due to social deprivation:A person is vulnerable due to social deprivation when he/she experiences at least one social deprivation, but has sufficient income to buy the aggregation of the basic food basket with the basic non-food basket.
  • Vulnerable due to income:A person is vulnerable due to income when he/she does not have sufficient income to buy the aggregation of the basic food basket with the basic non-food basket, but has no social deprivations.
